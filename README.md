## Abstract

The Strategic Independent Research Zone for Mars, designated SIRZ Mars, is a proposed multidisciplinary research programme for developing, validating, and operating the scientific and engineering systems required for sustained activity on Mars. Its principal technical focus is resilient surface energy, supported by in situ resource utilisation, autonomous operations, closed environment habitation, planetary science, human health protection, and rigorous planetary protection. The programme is structured as a lawful international research framework rather than a territorial or sovereign claim. The term research zone denotes a managed operational perimeter for safety, contamination control, instrumentation, logistics, and coordinated scientific activity. It does not denote ownership of Martian land.

SIRZ Mars develops the Strategic Independent Research Zone concept into a Mars-specific architecture. Established in 2025 as an independent research governance concept, the project subsequently evolved toward Mars and energy research. The present framework defines a unified Mars programme, a consistent institutional structure, and a clear separation between qualified mission systems and exploratory research. The proposed energy architecture combines firm surface power, supplementary solar generation, distributed storage, thermal management, fault-tolerant distribution, and black start capability. Nuclear fission is treated as the reference source of firm power because Mars surface operations must continue through darkness, dust events, seasonal variability, and reduced solar intensity. Solar generation remains a valuable secondary source and research platform. Experimental concepts, including the Live Core field-confined high-energy storage concept, are assigned to an independently reviewed research track and cannot become mission-critical until empirical evidence, safety qualification, and reproducible performance justify advancement.

The programme uses staged development beginning with requirements consolidation and laboratory validation, followed by terrestrial analogues, orbital and lunar precursor demonstrations where scientifically justified, uncrewed Mars deployment, and only then a possible crewed research installation. Advancement is controlled by evidence-based decision gates covering scientific merit, technical readiness, safety, planetary protection, legal compliance, cost credibility, and mission assurance. Governance is proposed through FSIRCE with independent scientific, ethical, safety, planetary protection, data, and audit functions. The resulting framework is intended to support credible research planning, transparent collaboration, and disciplined transition from concept to verified capability.

**Keywords:** Mars research, surface energy, fission power, solar power, energy storage, in situ resource utilisation, autonomous systems, planetary protection, systems engineering, scientific governance

## Executive Summary

SIRZ Mars is a research and technology development programme for establishing a durable scientific presence on Mars. It is not a proposal addressed to a particular government, organisation, investor, or individual. It is a general scientific framework intended for assessment by qualified researchers and institutions.

The project began on March 16, 2025. The initial SIRZ concept examined independent research governance in a terrestrial setting. The project then developed into a Mars-focused scientific programme centred on energy, resource utilisation, autonomous operations, habitation, and planetary science. The present document establishes the authoritative project definition for that direction.

This revised proposal establishes one coherent project definition. SIRZ means Strategic Independent Research Zone. SIRZ Mars is the programme. FSIRCE means Foundation for Strategic Independent Research and Civic Ethics. FSIRCE is the proposed institutional steward and is not represented as an already constituted legal entity. Scientific independence means protection of research integrity, publication standards, and multidisciplinary review. It does not mean exemption from law, state supervision, registration requirements, liability obligations, safety standards, or planetary protection.

The programme has six integrated purposes:

1. Develop dependable energy systems for continuous Mars surface operations.
2. Demonstrate resource extraction and processing that reduce dependence on transported consumables.
3. Establish safe, maintainable, and contamination controlled research infrastructure.
4. Enable autonomous operation during communication delays and interruptions.
5. Conduct high value planetary science while protecting Mars and Earth from harmful biological contamination.
6. Produce reproducible evidence, open technical baselines, and qualified technologies that can support future missions.

Energy is the organising technical theme. Every major Mars activity depends on power, including thermal control, communications, environmental control, water processing, oxygen production, scientific instruments, mobility, construction, maintenance, medical systems, and emergency recovery. The baseline architecture therefore uses a firm power source, supplementary solar arrays, distributed energy storage, segmented microgrids, thermal energy management, and independent emergency reserves. The exact generating capacity is not fixed prematurely. It will be derived from mission modes, load inventories, reliability targets, site conditions, crew size, resource processing demand, and growth allowances. Early robotic operations may require tens of kilowatts. An expanded research settlement with substantial resource processing could require hundreds of kilowatts or reach megawatt scale. Such values are planning ranges, not commitments, and must be replaced by verified load models during system definition.

The scientific programme is divided into three assurance classes. Class A contains flight essential systems with mature evidence and complete qualification. Class B contains noncritical demonstrations that may support operations but are not required for survival. Class C contains exploratory high-energy or high-uncertainty research that is physically separated, independently reviewed, and prohibited from connection to life-critical systems. The Live Core concept is assigned to Class C. Its proposed use of electromagnetic confinement, phase controlled coupling, and a high-energy rotating core requires experimental verification of containment stability, materials performance, control authority, thermal rejection, failure behaviour, and net system value. It may advance only through measured evidence and cannot be presented as a proven storage or propulsion technology.

The surface facility is conceived as a distributed research complex rather than one vulnerable structure. It includes landing and cargo zones, power generation areas, energy storage, communications, robotic maintenance, pressurised laboratories, habitation, medical capability, clean sample handling, contaminated material handling, resource processing, shielding, spare parts, and protected emergency capacity. Separation distances and barriers are determined by hazard analysis, plume effects, radiation protection, dust transport, contamination pathways, and maintenance access. The operational zone is mapped and coordinated for safety. It is not a property boundary.

The programme follows a gated roadmap. Initial phases consolidate requirements, test components, operate terrestrial analogues, and validate autonomous control. Later phases deploy uncrewed Mars assets to measure the local environment, demonstrate power, extract resources, construct protective infrastructure, and confirm maintainability. A crewed phase is considered only after the surface power system, environmental control, communications, medical support, return strategy, emergency shelter, planetary protection procedures, and logistics reserves have satisfied defined acceptance criteria.

The proposal is governed by evidence, transparency, and proportional security. Legitimate intellectual property may be protected for a limited period, but safety data, environmental findings, planetary protection information, and results required for independent verification cannot be suppressed for strategic advantage. Dual use research receives additional review. Research involving humans, animals, microorganisms, nuclear systems, high-energy equipment, or returned samples requires specialist approval under applicable law and recognised standards.

SIRZ Mars does not claim that the project is currently funded, incorporated, licensed, flight approved, or technically ready for Mars deployment. It provides a complete research architecture for reaching those states through verifiable work.

## 1. Project Definition

### 1.1 Name and identity

The full project name is Strategic Independent Research Zone for Mars. The concise designation is SIRZ Mars. The original SIRZ title is retained because it expresses the project objective of protecting scientific independence while organising research within a defined operational zone.

The word strategic refers to research selected for long-term importance to sustained Mars activity. Independent refers to scientific judgment protected from improper political, commercial, ideological, or institutional pressure. Research refers to systematic investigation, engineering development, demonstration, verification, and publication. Zone refers to a managed operational and scientific area. It does not indicate territorial sovereignty, exclusive ownership, or a claim to natural resources before lawful extraction.

### 1.2 Project origin and development

The project inception date is March 16, 2025, based on the earliest dated project package. The first version examined an Earth-based model for independent research governance. The project subsequently developed toward Mars and energy research. This revision formally establishes Mars as the project environment and energy systems as the primary technical focus.

The Mars programme retains the relevant principles of scientific independence, ethical oversight, shared responsibility, controlled access, and institutional continuity. These principles are applied within the physical conditions of Mars, international space law, planetary protection policy, and modern space mission assurance practice.

### 1.3 Purpose

The purpose of SIRZ Mars is to create a disciplined research programme that can produce validated technologies, operational methods, scientific data, and governance procedures for long-duration activity on Mars. The programme shall:

1. Define traceable mission and research requirements.
2. Test candidate technologies under increasingly representative conditions.
3. Separate survival-critical systems from experimental systems.
4. Create independent review and audit mechanisms.
5. Publish sufficient methods and evidence for scientific scrutiny.
6. Protect human participants, the terrestrial environment, and the Martian environment.
7. Coordinate research activities without asserting territorial rights.
8. Provide a credible basis for collaboration, funding, licensing, and mission partnership.

### 1.4 Scope boundaries

SIRZ Mars includes research on energy generation, storage, distribution, conversion, thermal rejection, resource processing, autonomous operations, habitation, health protection, construction, communications, planetary science, and planetary protection where these activities support Mars research operations.

The programme does not include unrestricted human enhancement, unbounded artificial intelligence research, weapons development, territorial acquisition, evasion of national regulation, unreviewed biological experimentation, or deployment of unverified high-energy systems near crewed infrastructure. Artificial intelligence is included only as a controlled enabling technology for autonomy, planning, fault detection, robotics, science operations, and human decision support.

### 1.5 Intended programme outputs

The programme is expected to produce:

1. A controlled requirements baseline.
2. A reference mission and surface architecture.
3. Verified energy system models and hardware demonstrations.
4. Site selection criteria and environmental datasets.
5. Resource utilisation demonstrations.
6. Autonomous operations software with documented assurance evidence.
7. Planetary protection and contamination control protocols.
8. Safety cases and hazard records.
9. Governance, publication, data, and intellectual property policies.
10. Cost, schedule, mass, power, and risk baselines at defined maturity gates.
11. Peer reviewed scientific publications and technical reports.
12. Decision records that document why technologies were accepted, deferred, or rejected.

## 2. Scientific and Strategic Rationale

### 2.1 Mars as a research environment

Mars offers exceptional scientific value because its geological record, climate history, water related features, surface chemistry, and potential past habitability can improve understanding of planetary evolution and the conditions associated with life. Research priorities for human exploration include geological investigation, climate science, geophysics, resource characterisation, life detection, sample science, human health, technology performance, and human interaction with autonomous systems (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2026).

Mars also imposes severe engineering constraints. Surface systems must withstand low atmospheric pressure, low-temperatures, dust, oxidising compounds, radiation, reduced gravity, long communication delays, limited opportunities for resupply, and difficult entry, descent, and landing. A Mars research facility must therefore be more autonomous, repairable, resource efficient, and fault-tolerant than a comparable terrestrial laboratory.

### 2.2 Energy as the central dependency

Energy is the common dependency across all surface functions. A loss of electrical or thermal power can disable environmental control, communications, oxygen production, water recovery, scientific instruments, medical equipment, mobility, computing, and maintenance. Energy architecture must therefore be treated as a system of generation, storage, conversion, distribution, thermal management, control, protection, maintenance, and recovery rather than as a single generator.

A credible architecture must operate through nominal conditions, dust accumulation, night, seasonal changes, equipment failure, maintenance periods, and emergency isolation. It must also support staged growth without replacing the entire initial system. NASA assessments have selected fission as the reference primary power source for initial crewed Mars concepts because firm generation is less dependent on sunlight and can support continuous loads. Solar power remains important as a complementary, distributed, and scalable source (National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 2024c).

### 2.3 Need for disciplined independence

Frontier research can be distorted by sponsor expectations, proprietary secrecy, publication pressure, technological enthusiasm, or institutional inertia. SIRZ Mars addresses these risks through plural governance, documented decisions, independent replication, conflicts of interest controls, and protected publication rights. Scientific independence is not achieved through isolation. It is achieved through transparent methods, qualified review, and a funding structure that prevents one sponsor from controlling results.

### 2.4 Need for phased evidence

Mars hardware cannot be validated by conceptual plausibility alone. The programme therefore uses progressive evidence. Analytical models are followed by component experiments, integrated laboratory systems, environmental testing, analogue operations, precursor missions, and operational demonstrations. Each phase tests explicit hypotheses and acceptance criteria. Advancement is not based on reputation, advocacy, or novelty.

## 3. Mission, Vision, and Principles

### 3.1 Mission

The mission of SIRZ Mars is to develop and validate energy-centred scientific infrastructure that enables safe, lawful, sustained, and internationally responsible research on Mars.

### 3.2 Vision

The long-term vision is a distributed Mars research complex capable of continuous scientific operation, progressive use of local resources, transparent international collaboration, and responsible growth without territorial appropriation or unacceptable environmental harm.

### 3.3 Governing principles

| Principle | Operational meaning |
|---|---|
| Scientific integrity | Methods, data provenance, uncertainty, negative results, and conflicts of interest are documented. |
| Human safety | Life-critical functions use qualified systems, redundancy, isolation, and verified emergency procedures. |
| Planetary protection | Forward and backward contamination risks are controlled through mission design, cleanliness, monitoring, and containment. |
| Legal compliance | Activities remain subject to the authorisation and continuing supervision of appropriate states and competent authorities. |

| Security proportionality | Access restrictions protect people, missions, sensitive data, and legitimate intellectual property without becoming a mechanism for suppressing adverse evidence. |
| International coordination | Operations are communicated and coordinated to reduce harmful interference and duplicate risk. |

## 4. Scientific Objectives and Research Questions

### 4.1 Primary objective

The primary objective is to demonstrate a resilient Mars surface energy system that can sustain scientific operations through expected environmental variation and credible equipment failures.

Primary research questions include:

1. What combination of firm generation, solar generation, storage, and load management provides the lowest mission risk for a defined surface campaign?
2. How should power assets be distributed to limit common-cause failure while controlling cabling mass, conversion losses, and maintenance burden?
3. What storage technologies remain reliable under Mars temperature cycles, dust exposure, radiation, low pressure, and long dormancy?
4. How can thermal energy be recovered, stored, transported, and rejected to improve total system efficiency?
5. What level of autonomy is required to detect, isolate, diagnose, and recover from faults without immediate Earth intervention?
6. How can energy intensive resource processing be scheduled without threatening life-critical loads?
7. Which components can be inspected, repaired, or manufactured using local materials?

### 4.2 Resource utilisation objective

The resource utilisation objective is to determine whether water, atmospheric carbon dioxide, regolith, and other accessible materials can be processed safely and efficiently to reduce transported mass.

Research questions include:

1. What local water forms are accessible at the selected site and what extraction energy is required?
2. What purification sequence is required for human use, electrolysis, industrial processes, and scientific cleanliness?
3. How should oxygen production from atmospheric carbon dioxide be scaled, maintained, and integrated with storage?
4. Which regolith processing methods can produce shielding, landing surfaces, construction feedstock, or useful metals without unacceptable dust and chemical exposure?
5. How can extracted resources be measured, recorded, and managed to support transparency and environmental assessment?

### 4.3 Habitation and human performance objective

The habitation objective is to protect health and maintain performance during long-duration isolation, radiation exposure, reduced gravity, constrained medical care, and delayed communication.

Research questions include:

1. What shielding configuration provides effective protection with acceptable mass and construction effort?
2. How can dust and perchlorate bearing material be excluded from pressurised volumes?
3. What environmental control redundancy is required for safe maintenance and recovery?
4. How should workload, sleep, exercise, privacy, team composition, and behavioural health support be designed?
5. Which medical conditions must be diagnosable and treatable locally?
6. How should human operators supervise autonomous systems without automation bias or loss of situational awareness?

### 4.4 Planetary science objective

The planetary science objective is to investigate Mars while preserving the integrity of the environment and scientific record.

Research questions include:

1. What does local geology reveal about the history of water, climate, volcanism, and habitability?
2. Which samples have the highest value for mineralogical, geochemical, chronological, and possible biosignature analysis?
3. How can surface and subsurface measurements distinguish indigenous signals from terrestrial contamination?
4. What long-duration observations can improve understanding of weather, dust, radiation, seismic activity, and subsurface structure?
5. How can human and robotic capabilities be combined to increase scientific return without compromising sample cleanliness?

### 4.5 Governance research objective

SIRZ Mars also treats governance as a research domain. The objective is to test whether independent review, open evidence, tiered access, and international coordination can support high-consequence science without territorial or institutional capture.

Research questions include:

1. Which governance structures best preserve scientific independence while ensuring accountability?
2. How should access, cost, risk, data, and publication rights be allocated among contributors?
3. What information must be public for safety and scientific verification?
4. How can conflicts between proprietary interests and planetary protection be resolved?
5. What audit evidence is necessary for public and institutional confidence?

## 5. Programme Architecture

### 5.1 Programme elements

SIRZ Mars is organised as a connected set of Earth, space, and Mars elements.

| Element | Function |
|---|---|
| Programme governance centre | Legal administration, finance, ethics, audit, agreements, records, and programme control. |
| Earth research laboratories | Component development, materials testing, energy research, biological containment, robotics, and software assurance. |
| Integrated analogue sites | Long-duration operations in environments that reproduce isolation, dust, cold, limited logistics, delayed support, and constrained power. |
| Communications and data segment | Secure command, telemetry, scientific data, time synchronisation, archiving, and delay tolerant networking. |
| Orbital support segment | Communications relay, navigation support, remote sensing, and contingency observation where mission architecture requires it. |
| Mars precursor segment | Site measurements, power demonstrations, resource prospecting, construction trials, and autonomous maintenance. |
| Mars surface research complex | Energy generation, laboratories, habitation, resource processing, maintenance, medical capability, and field science. |
| Independent assurance network | External review, replication, safety assessment, planetary protection review, and financial audit. |

### 5.2 Surface functional areas

The surface complex is divided by function and hazard rather than by institutional ownership.

1. Landing and ascent area, positioned to control plume, debris, traffic, and contamination effects.
2. Nuclear power area, separated according to launch safety, radiation, maintenance, and failure analysis.
3. Solar generation fields, divided into independently isolatable sections.
4. Energy storage and conversion area, with fire, pressure, chemical, and thermal barriers.
5. Primary habitation and medical area, protected by shielding and redundant environmental control.
6. Science laboratories, including geology, geochemistry, microscopy, environmental monitoring, and controlled sample processing.
7. Clean sample area for materials requiring strict biological and chemical cleanliness.
8. Resource processing area for water extraction, atmospheric processing, electrolysis, regolith handling, and waste treatment.
9. Robotics and maintenance area with machine tools, inspection systems, spare parts, and additive manufacturing.
10. Contaminated material area for dusty equipment and potentially hazardous chemical residues.
11. Emergency refuge with independent atmosphere, power, communications, water, medical supplies, and fire isolation.
12. Field science corridors and exclusion areas defined by safety and planetary protection requirements.

### 5.3 Distributed architecture

A single central structure creates common-cause vulnerability. SIRZ Mars therefore uses functional distribution with protected interconnection. Essential functions are duplicated or recoverable across physically separated modules. Fire, depressurisation, dust intrusion, power conversion faults, and contamination events must be capable of local isolation. Distribution must not create excessive maintenance burden or require exposed crew activity for routine switching.

### 5.4 Growth strategy

The architecture is designed for incremental deployment:

1. Robotic survey and environmental monitoring.
2. Initial firm power and communications.
3. Resource prospecting and small scale processing.
4. Construction and shielding demonstration.
5. Expanded power, storage, and maintenance capability.
6. Prepositioned crew consumables, spares, emergency shelter, and return assets.
7. Initial crewed research occupancy.
8. Expansion only after measured reliability and resource performance justify growth.

## 6. Research Portfolio and Assurance Classes

### 6.1 Classification method

Every experiment and technology is assigned an assurance class according to consequence, evidence, and operational dependence.

| Class | Definition | Permitted role |
|---|---|---|
| Class A | Qualified mission system supported by complete verification, safety evidence, configuration control, and operational acceptance. | May perform life-critical or mission-critical functions. |
| Class B | Demonstration system with bounded hazards, independent isolation, and an established fallback. | May support operations but cannot be required for crew survival or safe return. |
| Class C | Exploratory system with high-uncertainty, high-energy, novel physics, immature containment, or incomplete failure knowledge. | Research only, physically and functionally separated from critical infrastructure. |

### 6.2 Transition between classes

A technology cannot advance because of conceptual appeal alone. Transition requires:

1. Reproducible measurements.
2. Independent review of methods and uncertainty.
3. Defined operating envelope.
4. Characterised failure modes.
5. Verified containment and shutdown.
6. Environmental compatibility.
7. Maintainability evidence.
8. Cybersecurity and control assurance where software is involved.
9. Demonstrated value relative to mature alternatives.
10. Formal configuration and change control.

### 6.3 Portfolio balance

The research portfolio must maintain sufficient investment in enabling systems, maintenance, safety, and measurement. Novel energy concepts cannot consume resources required to qualify baseline power. Portfolio decisions are made using scientific merit, mission relevance, risk, cost, opportunity value, and availability of independent expertise.

## 7. Mars Surface Energy Architecture

### 7.1 Energy design objectives

The energy system shall:

1. Supply continuous power to life-critical and mission-critical loads.
2. Tolerate loss of any single ordinary generation or conversion unit without loss of safe habitat conditions.
3. Survive expected dust, temperature, radiation, and low pressure conditions.
4. Support autonomous fault detection, isolation, and recovery.
5. Permit maintenance without total system shutdown.
6. Protect emergency reserves from routine consumption.
7. Scale through modular additions.
8. Record power quality, energy balance, degradation, and failure data.
9. Prevent experimental systems from propagating faults into critical buses.
10. Provide verified black start capability after a complete distribution shutdown.

### 7.2 Reference generation mix

The reference architecture uses fission as firm generation and solar photovoltaic arrays as supplementary generation. This is a baseline for research and trade studies, not an irreversible procurement decision.

Fission offers continuous output independent of sunlight and dust opacity. Its challenges include launch safety, reactor control, shielding, heat rejection, deployment, maintenance strategy, end of life disposition, and public acceptance. Solar generation offers modularity, distributed placement, simple conversion, and useful daytime surplus. Its challenges include lower solar intensity than Earth, night, dust deposition, atmospheric opacity, seasonal effects, array area, cleaning, storage demand, and cable distribution.

The preferred solution is a hybrid system. Firm generation carries protected loads and a defined operating reserve. Solar power supplies variable scientific and industrial loads, charges storage, and reduces fuel and thermal burden where applicable. Load scheduling moves energy intensive processes to periods of surplus without compromising continuous functions.

### 7.3 Load categories

| Load category | Examples | Supply rule |
|---|---|---|
| Survival critical | Environmental control, pressure monitoring, carbon dioxide removal, essential thermal control, emergency lighting, medical life support | Supplied by protected buses with independent reserve and automatic shedding of lower priorities. |
| Mission-critical | Communications, command computing, navigation support, essential laboratory containment, water treatment, core maintenance systems | Supplied continuously with controlled interruption only under defined contingency procedures. |
| Operational | Mobility charging, routine laboratories, workshop systems, food production, nonessential computing | Scheduled and shed when required to preserve higher priorities. |
| Industrial | Water extraction, oxygen production beyond immediate need, regolith processing, manufacturing, large thermal processes | Operated primarily during available surplus and subject to production inventory targets. |
| Experimental | Novel storage, high-energy physics, prototype conversion systems | Isolated from critical buses and disabled automatically during abnormal system conditions. |

### 7.4 Capacity determination

Capacity is derived from an auditable load model. Each load entry includes nominal power, peak power, duty cycle, startup demand, thermal interaction, allowable interruption, recovery priority, growth allowance, and uncertainty. Mission modes include predeployment, dormancy, robotic operation, crew arrival, nominal occupancy, maintenance, science peak, industrial production, dust event, partial failure, medical emergency, and evacuation preparation.

The design shall calculate energy, not only instantaneous power. Storage duration is determined from credible outage scenarios, generator restart time, solar unavailability, maintenance duration, and emergency reserve policy. Capacity margins are allocated explicitly and cannot be consumed without configuration approval.

### 7.5 Storage technologies

No single storage technology is assumed to satisfy every timescale. Candidate layers include:

1. High cycle electrochemical batteries for transient support and bus stability.
2. Regenerative fuel cells for longer duration storage where reactant management is justified.
3. Thermal storage for habitat heating, industrial processes, and waste heat recovery.
4. Mechanical storage where mass, containment, bearing life, and vacuum operation are favourable.
5. Chemical energy reserves integrated with oxygen and fuel production.
6. Experimental electromagnetic or high-energy concepts confined to Class C research.

Storage selection must account for energy density, power density, cycle life, calendar life, temperature tolerance, self discharge, pressure vessels, fire behaviour, reactant leakage, radiation effects, maintainability, monitoring, and end of life handling.

### 7.6 Distribution and microgrids

The surface distribution system is segmented into independently controllable microgrids. Each segment has local protection, metering, fault detection, grounding strategy, isolation devices, and a defined reconnection protocol. Critical modules can operate in an islanded state. Power conversion units are replaceable and standardised where practical.

Cable routing must consider abrasive dust, thermal contraction, vehicle traffic, excavation, radiation, connector reliability, and repair access. Buried or covered routes may protect cables but complicate fault location. Above surface routes simplify inspection but increase exposure. The final method is selected by test and site conditions.

### 7.7 Control and autonomy

Energy management software shall forecast generation, demand, storage state, maintenance needs, and resource production. It may optimise schedules within authorised bounds, but it cannot silently alter safety limits. Safety functions use deterministic and independently verifiable protection where appropriate. Machine learning may support anomaly detection, degradation prediction, and planning, but life-critical shutdown and protection logic require transparent, testable behaviour.

The control architecture shall include:

1. Local autonomous protection.
2. Supervisory energy management.
3. Human command authority.
4. Safe degraded modes.
5. Secure time synchronisation.
6. Tamper evident logs.
7. Simulation and digital twin support.
8. Recovery from corrupted or unavailable central control.

### 7.8 Thermal integration

Electrical power and thermal control are designed together. Fission and industrial processes may provide recoverable heat. Habitats, greenhouses, water systems, batteries, electronics, and laboratories have different thermal requirements. Heat transport must prevent freezing, overheating, and cross contamination. Radiators must operate through dust exposure and changing environmental conditions. Waste heat is used where safe and efficient, but no life-critical function may depend on an unqualified industrial process.

### 7.9 Black start and emergency power

Black start is the ability to restore the system when the main distribution network is deenergised. SIRZ Mars requires protected startup sources, locally stored procedures, independent communications, and rehearsed recovery sequences. Emergency power is physically protected, monitored, and reserved for survival-critical functions. Routine operations cannot draw down emergency capacity below the approved threshold.

### 7.10 Energy research test programme

The energy programme shall test:

1. Dust deposition and removal on solar arrays and radiators.
2. Low temperature battery performance and thermal conditioning.
3. Long-duration cable, connector, and switchgear reliability.
4. Fission power deployment interfaces and heat rejection.
5. Microgrid fault isolation and controlled reconnection.
6. Storage degradation under representative cycling.
7. Power electronics under radiation and thermal stress.
8. Autonomous load shedding and recovery.
9. Resource processing under variable power.
10. Maintenance with limited tools, spares, and crew time.
11. Emergency reserve preservation.
12. Multi year operation with configuration changes.

## 8. Live Core Research Track

### 8.1 Concept status

The Live Core concept proposes field-confined high-energy storage and phase-steered coupling using a rotating solid or plasma core suspended or controlled by shaped electromagnetic fields in a vacuum environment (Farhangi, 2026). The concept is a research hypothesis published as a preprint. It is not a demonstrated Mars power source, storage device, or propulsion system.

For programme governance, Live Core shall be treated as Class C and below technology readiness level three until experiments demonstrate proof of critical functions. This conservative classification protects the scientific credibility of the broader programme and prevents mission architecture from depending on an unverified concept.

### 8.2 Research hypotheses

The research track shall test the following hypotheses:

1. A candidate core can be stably confined or supported within a defined operating envelope.
2. Active control can suppress destabilising motion with bounded power and latency.
3. Energy can be introduced, stored, measured, and extracted with a useful round-trip efficiency.
4. Containment remains stable under predictable disturbances.
5. Failure energy can be contained without catastrophic fragment, plasma, magnetic, thermal, or electrical effects.
6. Materials, coils, bearings where applicable, insulation, sensors, and power electronics can survive the expected duty cycle.
7. The complete system offers a measurable advantage over conventional storage after accounting for containment, cooling, control, shielding, and safety mass.

### 8.3 Experimental sequence

| Stage | Experiment | Advancement evidence |
|---|---|---|
| LC 1 | Analytical model and simulation benchmark | Conserved quantities, uncertainty bounds, numerical convergence, and independent model review. |
| LC 2 | Low-energy benchtop confinement | Repeatable stable operation, calibrated measurements, and safe shutdown. |
| LC 3 | Disturbance response testing | Demonstrated control authority and bounded response to defined perturbations. |
| LC 4 | Charge and discharge measurement | Verified energy accounting and uncertainty analysis. |
| LC 5 | Component endurance | Measured degradation over representative cycles. |
| LC 6 | Containment failure testing | Confirmed barriers, exclusion distances, and passive safe state. |
| LC 7 | Integrated prototype | Independent replication and comparison with conventional storage. |
| LC 8 | Relevant environment demonstration | Performance under vacuum, thermal cycling, radiation, and remotely operated maintenance. |

### 8.4 Prohibited dependencies

Live Core shall not:

1. Supply a survival-critical bus.
2. Share an unprotected containment volume with habitation.
3. Operate without independent shutdown and energy dissipation.
4. Be described as validated propulsion without measured thrust, complete energy accounting, and independent replication.
5. Proceed to higher energy before the preceding containment stage is accepted.
6. Use Mars deployment as the first integrated safety test.

### 8.5 Scientific publication

Methods, calibration, uncertainty, negative findings, and failure results shall be published after legitimate safety and intellectual property review. Claims shall distinguish stored energy, recoverable energy, input energy, control energy, thermal losses, and total system mass. Independent replication is required before mission relevance can be asserted.

## 9. In Situ Resource Utilisation

### 9.1 Role in the programme

In situ resource utilisation reduces dependence on transported mass but creates new energy, maintenance, contamination, and reliability demands. It shall therefore supplement rather than prematurely replace essential reserves. Initial crews must not depend on an unverified local resource process for immediate survival or return.

### 9.2 Atmospheric oxygen production

Mars atmospheric carbon dioxide can be processed to produce oxygen. The Mars Oxygen In Situ Resource Utilization Experiment demonstrated oxygen production from the Martian atmosphere at small scale, reaching approximately 12 grams per hour and high purity during its highest output operations (Hoffman et al., 2022; National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 2023b). Scaling from an experiment to operational production requires larger compressors, reactors, filters, thermal control, storage, maintenance, and redundancy.

SIRZ Mars shall investigate:

1. Oxygen production efficiency across atmospheric density and temperature conditions.
2. Dust filtration and compressor wear.
3. Cell degradation and replacement.
4. Startup and shutdown energy.
5. Oxygen purity for breathing, combustion, laboratory use, and propulsion.
6. Safe compression and storage.
7. Integration with hydrogen obtained from water.
8. Inventory rules that preserve emergency reserves.

### 9.3 Water prospecting and extraction

Near surface water ice is a major site selection factor because water can support drinking, hygiene, food production, oxygen generation, hydrogen production, thermal storage, radiation shielding, and industrial processes. Remote sensing indicates accessible ice in multiple regions, but local concentration, depth, mechanical properties, contamination, and seasonal behaviour require direct measurement (National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 2023a).

The programme shall use staged prospecting:

1. Orbital mapping and terrain analysis.
2. Surface geophysics and neutron measurements.
3. Sterile or contamination controlled drilling where required.
4. Sample characterisation.
5. Pilot extraction with complete energy accounting.
6. Water purification and quality verification.
7. Reservoir monitoring and environmental assessment.

### 9.4 Regolith use

Regolith may provide shielding, berms, landing surface stabilisation, construction feedstock, thermal mass, and mineral resources. It also presents dust, abrasion, electrostatic, and chemical hazards. Perchlorate and other reactive compounds require controlled handling, exposure limits, and cleaning methods.

Candidate applications shall be tested for structural properties, outgassing, chemical compatibility, dust release, thermal behaviour, radiation attenuation, production energy, and repairability. Construction methods should minimise crew exposure through robotic excavation, transport, compaction, sintering, or additive manufacturing.

### 9.5 Resource accounting

All extracted material shall be measured by location, method, mass, quality, use, waste, and environmental effect. Resource records support science, operational planning, coordination, and future legal obligations. Extraction shall remain proportionate to research and operational needs and shall not be used to imply ownership of unextracted deposits.

## 10. Habitation, Life Support, and Human Health

### 10.1 Habitat requirements

The habitat shall provide pressure integrity, atmosphere management, water recovery, thermal control, hygiene, food storage, waste management, fire protection, communications, medical capability, exercise, work areas, privacy, and emergency refuge. Each life support function requires monitoring, fault detection, maintenance access, spare strategy, and a safe degraded mode.

### 10.2 Environmental control

Environmental control and life support shall use layered recovery rather than a single closed loop assumption. Water and oxygen recovery targets must be justified by reliability and maintenance evidence. Stored reserves remain necessary for commissioning, contamination events, repair, and evacuation. Atmospheric trace contaminants are monitored continuously. Critical sensors use diverse measurement methods where common-mode error could threaten life.

### 10.3 Radiation protection

Radiation exposure occurs during transit and on the surface. Measurements from the Mars Science Laboratory mission demonstrate that galactic cosmic radiation and solar particle events are material design constraints (Hassler et al., 2014; Zeitlin et al., 2013). Protection combines mission timing, forecasting, vehicle and habitat design, local shielding material, exposure monitoring, operational limits, and a storm shelter.

SIRZ Mars shall maintain individual dosimetry, area monitoring, cumulative records, and exposure planning. Shielding trades must consider secondary particle production, structural mass, construction effort, habitat geometry, and emergency access. Exposure shall be kept as low as reasonably achievable within applicable standards and mission constraints.

### 10.4 Dust and chemical exposure

Martian dust can affect lungs, eyes, skin, seals, bearings, filters, electronics, solar arrays, radiators, and scientific cleanliness. Dust may contain reactive and toxic compounds. The architecture therefore uses external suit storage where feasible, airlocks with staged cleaning, electrostatic or mechanical dust removal, controlled pressure gradients, washable surfaces, local extraction, filter monitoring, and separate dirty maintenance areas.

Exposure control requires validated sampling and toxicology rather than reliance on visual cleanliness. Medical surveillance shall include respiratory, ocular, dermatological, and neurological indicators relevant to identified dust constituents.

### 10.5 Reduced gravity and physical health

Mars gravity is lower than Earth gravity. Long term effects on bone, muscle, cardiovascular function, balance, reproduction, and development remain uncertain. Crew operations shall include exercise, nutrition, monitoring, and research protocols reviewed by appropriate human research ethics bodies. No claim is made that exercise alone eliminates reduced gravity risk.

### 10.6 Behavioural health and team performance

Isolation, confinement, workload, delayed communication, danger, monotony, interpersonal conflict, and limited privacy can impair performance. Habitat and operations design shall provide private space, predictable schedules, meaningful recreation, communication opportunities, sleep protection, team training, conflict management, and confidential behavioural health support.

Crew selection shall consider technical competence, adaptability, communication, judgement, and cooperative behaviour. Psychological screening cannot be treated as a complete control. Organisational design, workload, leadership, and habitat quality remain critical.

### 10.7 Medical autonomy

Communication delay and evacuation limits require local diagnostic and treatment capability. Medical planning shall define:

1. Conditions that can be treated locally.
2. Conditions requiring stabilisation until return.
3. Conditions that cannot be treated with available capability.
4. Required imaging, laboratory, pharmaceutical, surgical, dental, and rehabilitation resources.
5. Medical consumable life and storage conditions.
6. Crew medical training.
7. Decision support that remains usable without Earth communication.
8. Ethical procedures for scarce resources and incapacity.

### 10.8 Human research protections

Research involving crew members requires informed consent, independent ethics review, privacy protection, minimisation of coercion, adverse event reporting, and clear separation between operational command and research participation decisions. Participation in nonessential research cannot be required as an informal condition of employment or mission status.

## 11. Robotics, Autonomy, Communications, and Data

### 11.1 Operational need

Earth and Mars communication delay varies and can reach many minutes in one direction. Solar conjunction can further restrict communication. Surface systems must therefore perform local protection, routine planning, navigation, maintenance support, and scientific triage without continuous Earth control (National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 2023c).

### 11.2 Robotic roles

Robots shall perform tasks that are hazardous, repetitive, distant, dusty, or required before crew arrival. Functions include surveying, construction, cable deployment, array cleaning, inspection, cargo movement, drilling, sample collection, maintenance support, emergency reconnaissance, and external science.

Robotic systems must be recoverable when practical. Designs shall include accessible lifting points, standard connectors, manual override, localisation alternatives, safe stop behaviour, and procedures for towing or abandonment.

### 11.3 Autonomy assurance

Autonomous systems are approved for defined decision envelopes. Assurance evidence includes training data provenance where applicable, simulation coverage, hardware testing, off-nominal scenarios, cybersecurity analysis, explainable alerts, operator workload evaluation, and independent verification.

Artificial intelligence shall not have unrestricted authority over life support, nuclear safety, medical decisions, research ethics, crew discipline, or mission termination. Decision support may recommend actions, but authority and responsibility remain assigned to accountable humans and qualified protection systems.

### 11.4 Human and agent teaming

The programme shall study allocation of tasks between people and autonomous agents. Research shall measure trust calibration, workload, error detection, mode awareness, intervention timing, communication quality, and performance under degraded conditions. The goal is neither maximum automation nor maximum manual control. The goal is a verified division of responsibility that improves safety and scientific return.

### 11.5 Communications architecture

Communications shall use redundant local networks, surface relays, orbital relay where available, delay tolerant protocols, prioritised traffic, error correction, secure command authentication, and local archives. Essential procedures, technical manuals, medical knowledge, maps, and software recovery images must remain available locally.

### 11.6 Data governance

Every scientific dataset shall include provenance, calibration, time, location, instrument state, processing history, uncertainty, access classification, and retention policy. Raw data are preserved when feasible. Derived data must be traceable to source measurements. Changes to algorithms and calibration are version controlled.

Data are classified into:

1. Open scientific data.
2. Controlled personal or medical data.
3. Safety sensitive technical data.
4. Legitimate time-limited proprietary data.
5. Security restricted information governed by applicable law.

Classification decisions are reviewable and cannot be used to hide research misconduct, environmental harm, or safety deficiencies.

### 11.7 Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity protects command, life support, energy, robotics, scientific integrity, and personal information. Controls include network segmentation, least privilege, authenticated updates, secure boot, cryptographic integrity, offline recovery, anomaly monitoring, incident response, and supply chain assurance. Safety systems shall retain safe local behaviour during loss or compromise of higher level networks.

## 12. Planetary Science Programme

### 12.1 Science strategy

The science programme shall be selected through peer review and aligned with recognised Mars science priorities. It shall combine regional field investigations with long-duration observatories and laboratory analysis. Human presence is justified scientifically only where it provides capabilities that cannot be achieved as safely or efficiently by robotic means.

### 12.2 Core disciplines

| Discipline | Research focus |
|---|---|
| Geology and geochemistry | Stratigraphy, mineralogy, aqueous alteration, volcanism, impact history, and chronology. |
| Astrobiology | Habitability, organic chemistry, potential biosignatures, contamination discrimination, and life detection methods. |
| Climate and atmosphere | Dust, clouds, water cycle, trace gases, boundary layer processes, and long-term weather records. |
| Geophysics | Seismic activity, interior structure, heat flow, magnetic properties, and subsurface layering. |
| Hydrology and cryosphere | Distribution, state, origin, movement, and accessibility of water ice and hydrated materials. |
| Space environment | Surface radiation, solar events, atmospheric shielding, and effects on equipment and biology. |
| Human exploration science | Health, performance, operations, maintenance, autonomy, resource use, and habitat interaction. |

### 12.3 Sample science

Sample collection shall use documented context, contamination controls, chain of custody, instrument blanks, witness materials, and archive samples. Potential biosignature samples require higher cleanliness and containment. Scientific value shall be protected from operational convenience. Samples intended for possible Earth return require compliance with applicable backward contamination requirements and a separately approved containment architecture.

### 12.4 Long-duration observatories

The facility should support continuous environmental and geophysical measurements. Long records can reveal seasonal and interannual variability that short missions cannot observe. Instruments may include weather stations, radiation detectors, seismometers, subsurface sensors, dust monitors, atmospheric spectrometers, and astronomical or solar observations where site conditions permit.

### 12.5 Science selection

Research proposals are evaluated for scientific significance, method quality, feasibility, resource demand, contamination risk, crew time, data value, and relationship to existing work. Mission prestige and sponsor status do not substitute for scientific merit.

## 14. Systems Engineering and Verification

### 14.1 Systems engineering approach

SIRZ Mars shall use a life cycle systems engineering process consistent with established space mission practice (National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 2016). The process connects stakeholder needs, mission concepts, requirements, architecture, design, verification, validation, operations, and disposal.

### 14.2 Requirements hierarchy

Requirements are organised as:

1. Programme requirements.
2. Mission requirements.
3. Surface system requirements.
4. Element requirements.
5. Subsystem requirements.
6. Interface requirements.
7. Verification requirements.
8. Operational constraints.

Each requirement must be necessary, unambiguous, verifiable, traceable, feasible, and assigned to an owner. Terms such as adequate, rapid, advanced, safe, and reliable require measurable definitions.

### 14.3 Architecture trades

Major trades include:

1. Fission capacity and unit count.
2. Solar fraction and array distribution.
3. Storage duration and technology mix.
4. Habitat location and shielding method.
5. Centralised compared with distributed resource processing.
6. Surface mobility fleet.
7. Orbital relay dependence.
8. Cargo sequence and redundancy.
9. Local manufacturing scope.
10. Crew size and mission duration.
11. Mars ascent and return architecture.
12. Planetary protection strategy.

Trade studies shall state assumptions, criteria, weighting, uncertainty, sensitivity, and residual risk. A preferred option is not accepted when a small change in an uncertain assumption reverses the result without further investigation.

### 14.4 Model based engineering

A controlled digital system model shall connect requirements, interfaces, mass, power, thermal performance, reliability, hazards, verification, and operations. Models are validated against test data and version controlled. A digital twin may support operations, but it is not considered accurate without continuous configuration and calibration control.

### 14.5 Verification and validation

Verification determines whether the system was built according to requirements. Validation determines whether it satisfies the intended mission need. Methods include analysis, inspection, demonstration, test, and similarity where justified. Critical requirements should not rely only on analysis when representative testing is feasible.

### 14.6 Environmental testing

Qualification shall address:

1. Vibration and shock.
2. Vacuum and low pressure.
3. Thermal cycling.
4. Radiation.
5. Dust and abrasion.
6. Electromagnetic compatibility.
7. Long-duration operation.
8. Dormancy and restart.
9. Fault injection.
10. Software and cybersecurity stress.
11. Maintenance in restricted access conditions.
12. Integrated mission scenarios.

### 14.7 Configuration control

Hardware, software, procedures, models, and data definitions are placed under configuration control before qualification. Changes require documented rationale, impact analysis, approval, implementation evidence, and regression verification. Uncontrolled field modifications are prohibited except under emergency authority, followed by formal review and records.

### 14.8 Reliability, maintainability, and logistics

Reliability analysis shall include common-cause failures, dependent failures, latent faults, human error, software failure, and environmental degradation. Maintainability is tested with realistic tools, gloves, visibility, time pressure, communication delay, and available spares. Logistics models shall include consumption, failure distributions, repair yield, shelf life, cannibalisation policy, and manufacturing capability.

### 15.1 Institutional structure

FSIRCE is proposed as the institutional steward for SIRZ Mars. The consistent name is Foundation for Strategic Independent Research and Civic Ethics. Its legal form, jurisdiction of incorporation, charitable or public benefit status, and governing documents must be established before it enters binding agreements or receives restricted funds.

### 15.3 Independence safeguards

1. No single sponsor may appoint a controlling majority of the Governing Board.
2. Scientific review members shall disclose financial, institutional, and personal conflicts.
3. Safety and planetary protection authorities shall have independent stop work power.
4. Researchers shall have a protected route to report misconduct or safety concerns.
5. Adverse results shall not be suppressed because they reduce commercial value.
6. Audit findings and corrective actions shall be tracked to closure.
7. Governance terms, voting rules, and removal procedures shall be public unless restricted by applicable law.

### 15.4 International space law

SIRZ Mars shall operate consistently with the Outer Space Treaty and other applicable international obligations. The legal baseline includes the following principles:

1. Exploration and use of outer space shall be carried out for the benefit and in the interests of all countries.
2. Mars is not subject to national appropriation by sovereignty, use, occupation, or other means.
3. States bear international responsibility for national space activities, including nongovernmental activities, and must provide authorisation and continuing supervision.
4. A state of registry retains jurisdiction and control over registered space objects and personnel as provided by law.
5. Activities shall be conducted with due regard for the interests of other states and with appropriate consultation where harmful interference may occur.
6. Harmful contamination of celestial bodies and adverse changes to the environment of Earth from extraterrestrial matter must be avoided.
7. Registration and liability obligations must be allocated through participating states and contracts.

These principles arise from the Treaty on Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space, Including the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies, the Convention on International Liability for Damage Caused by Space Objects, and the Convention on Registration of Objects Launched into Outer Space (United Nations, 1967, 1972, 1975).

### 15.5 Meaning of independence under law

Operational independence means that SIRZ Mars may maintain its own research governance, technical standards, access procedures, and publication protections within the authority granted by responsible states and contractual partners. It does not mean legal immunity. Personnel, space objects, nuclear sources, communications, biological materials, launches, reentries, samples, and financial activities remain subject to applicable jurisdictions.

### 15.6 Agreements

Participation agreements shall define:

1. Authorising states and responsible entities.
2. Ownership and registration of space objects.
3. Liability allocation and insurance.
4. Intellectual property and data rights.
5. Safety and planetary protection obligations.
6. Export control and sanctions compliance.
7. Resource contributions and cost responsibility.
8. Decision rights and dispute resolution.
9. Crew authority and emergency command.
10. Termination, withdrawal, and continuity of safety duties.
11. Publication rights and classification review.
12. Environmental monitoring and incident reporting.

### 15.7 Resource activity

Resource collection shall be conducted under applicable national authorisation and international obligations. The programme shall distinguish extracted material under lawful control from unextracted deposits and terrain. Operational coordination does not create exclusive territorial rights. Scientific sites of exceptional value may receive protective operating rules based on contamination and interference risk, not ownership.

## 16. Planetary Protection and Environmental Stewardship

### 16.1 Policy basis

Planetary protection is integral to mission design. The Committee on Space Research planetary protection policy provides the principal international scientific framework for controlling forward and backward contamination. Human missions require additional controls because people, habitats, waste, vehicles, and biological systems create contamination pathways that are more complex than those of many robotic missions (Committee on Space Research, 2024).

### 16.2 Forward contamination control

Forward contamination controls protect Mars and the scientific validity of life detection research. Measures shall include:

1. Biological burden assessment by hardware function and destination.
2. Clean assembly and monitored transport.
3. Sterilisation or reduction methods compatible with hardware.
4. Recontamination control after treatment.
5. Microbial inventory and molecular characterisation.
6. Controlled waste storage and treatment.
7. Restricted access to sensitive scientific regions.
8. Tracking of crew, vehicle, tool, and sample movement.
9. Environmental monitoring around the facility.
10. Incident response for unintended release.

### 16.3 Backward contamination control

Any material returned to Earth that has not been demonstrated safe requires a dedicated containment and assessment process. Sample return planning must define containment throughout launch from Mars, transit, Earth entry, recovery, transport, receiving facility operations, analysis, storage, and waste disposal. Safeguarding Earth has priority over schedule and sample access.

### 16.4 Human mission contamination zones

Human operations require separation between habitation, routine field science, life detection science, waste handling, resource processing, and returned sample preparation. Cleanliness levels are based on scientific and biological risk. A general habitat clean standard cannot substitute for the higher controls required for sensitive astrobiology.

### 16.5 Waste management

Waste is classified as biological, chemical, radioactive, electronic, metallic, polymeric, mineral, or mixed. The preferred hierarchy is prevention, reuse, recovery, safe storage, and controlled disposal. Release to the environment requires evidence that the action is lawful, scientifically justified, and consistent with planetary protection. Waste locations, composition, and condition are recorded.

### 16.6 Environmental monitoring

The programme shall establish baseline measurements before major operations. Monitoring includes dust, atmospheric chemistry, microbial indicators, radiation, local thermal effects, surface disturbance, resource extraction effects, and contamination markers. Results shall be preserved for comparison across the operating life of the facility.

## 17. Safety, Security, and Mission Assurance

### 17.1 Safety management

Safety is managed through a documented safety case. The safety case explains the system, hazards, controls, evidence, residual risk, responsibilities, and operational limits. It is maintained as the design changes.

### 17.2 Hazard management process

1. Identify hazards and initiating events.
2. Analyse causes, consequences, and affected functions.
3. Eliminate hazards where feasible.
4. Apply engineered controls.
5. Add detection, isolation, and recovery.
6. Establish procedures and training.
7. Verify control effectiveness.
8. Assign residual risk acceptance to an authorised level independent of the design team.
9. Monitor leading and lagging indicators.
10. Reopen analysis after incidents, design changes, or new evidence.

### 17.3 Principal hazards

The programme hazard register shall include:

1. Launch and entry failure.
2. Hard landing or cargo dispersion.
3. Reactor launch accident and surface malfunction.
4. Electrical arc, battery fire, and stored energy release.
5. Habitat depressurisation.
6. Internal fire and toxic combustion products.
7. Environmental control failure.
8. Radiation event.
9. Dust and chemical exposure.
10. Medical emergency.
11. Food or water contamination.
12. Loss of communications and navigation.
13. Autonomous system error.
14. Cyber intrusion or corrupted software.
15. Vehicle immobilisation or crew separation.
16. Crane, excavation, and construction hazards.
17. Pressure vessel rupture.
18. High-energy experiment failure.
19. Biological contamination.
20. Supply shortfall and spare exhaustion.
21. Human error, fatigue, conflict, and impaired judgement.
22. Mars ascent or return system unavailability.

### 17.4 Nuclear safety

Nuclear systems require compliance with launching state requirements, nuclear safety analysis, launch accident evaluation, reactor startup controls, radiation protection, security, emergency response, and end of life planning. Reactor location and shielding are selected by integrated analysis of radiation, cable length, maintenance access, thermal effects, terrain, plume interactions, and credible failure.

### 17.5 High-energy experiments

High-energy experiments require independent review of maximum credible energy, fragment and plasma behaviour, magnetic fields, electromagnetic interference, stored electrical energy, pressure, vacuum vessel failure, thermal release, fire, and remote shutdown. Experiments are located so that a complete containment failure does not disable habitation, firm power, communications, or return capability.

### 17.6 Emergency architecture

The emergency architecture shall include:

1. Protected refuge with independent atmosphere and power.
2. Multiple egress routes where structure permits.
3. Fire compartments and isolation.
4. Local breathing and medical supplies.
5. Independent communications.
6. Surface rescue capability.
7. Safe haven agreements with compatible nearby missions where possible.
8. Preplanned shelter during solar particle events.
9. Emergency power and water reserves.
10. Decision criteria for mission curtailment or evacuation.

### 17.7 Security

Security protects people, dangerous systems, mission continuity, controlled data, and scientific integrity. It shall remain proportionate and subject to oversight. Security shall not be used to conceal incidents, suppress legitimate criticism, or deny independent safety review.

## 18. Research Integrity, Publication, and Intellectual Property

### 18.1 Research integrity policy

The programme prohibits fabrication, falsification, plagiarism, undisclosed conflicts, inappropriate authorship, selective omission of adverse data, and manipulation of peer review. Allegations are assessed by an independent process with protection for good-faith reporting and procedural fairness for all parties.

### 18.2 Preregistration and protocols

High consequence experiments shall publish or securely register hypotheses, methods, primary outcomes, exclusion criteria, and analysis plans before data review where practical. Deviations are documented. Exploratory findings are identified as exploratory.

### 18.3 Reproducibility

Major claims require access to sufficient methods, calibration, code, uncertainty, and data for independent evaluation. When raw data cannot be openly released because of privacy, export control, security, or legitimate intellectual property, controlled access or independent replication shall be provided.

### 18.4 Publication protection

Researchers retain the right to publish scientifically valid results after a defined review for personal data, safety, legal restrictions, and patent filing. Review periods shall be limited. Sponsors cannot indefinitely delay publication because findings are unfavourable.

### 18.5 Intellectual property

Intellectual property rules shall reward genuine invention while preserving mission safety and scientific verification. Agreements shall define background intellectual property, project intellectual property, licensing, access for mission continuity, rights after partner withdrawal, and treatment of jointly developed work.

Safety critical interfaces, emergency procedures, contamination findings, and information necessary to prevent serious harm shall not be withheld solely for commercial advantage.

### 18.6 Authorship and credit

Authorship requires substantial intellectual contribution, participation in drafting or critical revision, approval of the final work, and accountability. Funding, technical assistance, operations, and data provision are acknowledged according to contribution. Institutional status alone does not justify authorship.

3. Consumables and spares prepositioned with verified inventories.
4. Mars ascent and return strategy accepted.
5. Local resource systems proven but not required for immediate survival unless fully qualified.
6. Planetary protection and sample handling procedures operational.
7. Independent safety review recommends proceeding.

### 20.4 Stop criteria

The programme shall pause or terminate an activity when:

1. Evidence contradicts a central technical assumption.
2. Residual risk exceeds authorised limits.
3. Planetary protection cannot be maintained.
4. Required legal authority is absent.
5. Cost growth removes the scientific or mission value.
6. Independent replication fails repeatedly.
7. A safer mature alternative makes the activity unjustified.
8. Governance interference compromises research integrity.

## 22. Cost, Funding, and Procurement Governance

### 22.1 Cost position

A responsible total mission cost cannot be stated before the reference architecture, crew assumptions, cargo sequence, power level, launch model, site, technology maturity, qualification programme, and partnership structure are defined. Publishing an unsupported total would create false precision.

The programme shall develop cost in stages. Early research budgets are based on defined work packages and test facilities. Mission costs are baselined at Preliminary Design Review after mass, power, schedule, procurement, risk, and reserve assumptions are traceable.

### 22.2 Cost structure

Cost accounts shall include:

1. Programme management and governance.
2. Scientific research and peer review.
3. Engineering labour.
4. Hardware and software development.
5. Test facilities and analogue campaigns.
6. Nuclear safety and licensing.
7. Launch and transportation.
8. Communications and ground operations.
9. Mars surface operations.
10. Spares, maintenance, and logistics.
11. Planetary protection and sample containment.
12. Independent assurance and audit.
13. Insurance and liability arrangements.
14. Education and public scientific communication.
15. Reserves for identified and unknown risk.
16. Decommissioning and long-term stewardship.

### 22.3 Funding principles

Funding shall be diversified to protect independence. Potential sources include public research grants, institutional partnerships, philanthropic science funding, mission contributions, competitive technology programmes, and carefully governed commercial licences. Funding agreements shall disclose conditions and conflicts.

Funds cannot purchase favourable scientific conclusions, exemption from safety review, control over adverse data, or access beyond legal and operational capacity.

### 22.4 Procurement

Procurement shall evaluate technical compliance, reliability, supply chain resilience, total life cycle cost, maintainability, cybersecurity, export controls, quality systems, and conflicts of interest. Critical components should not depend on a single undocumented supplier when alternatives or licensed manufacturing are feasible.

### 22.5 Financial controls

Controls include segregated duties, competitive tendering where appropriate, documented sole source justification, asset records, milestone based payment, independent audit, fraud reporting, and public summaries of major expenditure. Cost and schedule variance shall be reported with causes and corrective action.

## 25. Assumptions, Limitations, and Nonclaims

### 25.1 Assumptions

This framework assumes that future participating entities can obtain lawful launch, operation, communications, nuclear, research, and return authorisations. It assumes access to qualified launch services, deep space communications, and a credible Mars transportation architecture. It also assumes that precursor data can be collected before crew deployment.

### 25.2 Technical limitations

Many technologies required for a sustained Mars research complex have not operated together on Mars. Long term human response to Mars gravity is unknown. Local water accessibility varies by site. Dust toxicity and equipment effects require further characterisation. High power fission surface systems require development and approval. Large scale resource utilisation, construction, closed environment agriculture, and autonomous maintenance require substantial validation.

### 25.3 Schedule limitations

The phase ranges are planning tools. They are sensitive to funding, partner decisions, launch availability, technical results, legal approvals, and mission opportunities. The project shall not maintain an obsolete date by reducing safety or verification.

### 25.4 Cost limitations

No total implementation cost is claimed. Cost confidence requires a selected architecture and technical baseline. Early estimates shall include uncertainty and reserve and shall distinguish research expenditure from flight programme expenditure.

### 25.5 Legal limitations

This document is a research proposal, not legal advice, a licence, a treaty, a property instrument, or evidence of state authorisation. Detailed legal opinions are required for each participating jurisdiction and mission element.

### 25.6 Scientific nonclaims

SIRZ Mars does not claim:

1. Discovery of life on Mars.
2. A proven method for permanent human settlement.
3. A proven Live Core device.
4. Propulsion without propellant.
5. Existing ownership of Martian land or resources.
6. Exemption from national or international law.
7. Guaranteed funding, launch, partnership, or schedule.
8. Elimination of human health risk.
9. Complete closure of life support or resource cycles.
10. Scientific superiority based solely on institutional independence.

## 27. Conclusion

SIRZ Mars provides a coherent scientific and engineering framework for transforming the Strategic Independent Research Zone concept into a credible Mars research programme. Its defining contribution is not a claim of territory or regulatory exemption. Its contribution is the integration of scientific independence, energy resilience, staged verification, planetary protection, systems engineering, and accountable governance.

The programme places energy at the centre because every sustained Mars activity depends on reliable electrical and thermal power. It adopts a hybrid reference architecture with firm fission generation, supplementary solar generation, distributed storage, segmented microgrids, thermal integration, autonomous protection, and verified emergency recovery. It treats local resource use as a measured industrial system rather than an assumption. It protects crew health through layered environmental control, radiation protection, dust exclusion, medical autonomy, and human factors research. It protects scientific integrity through contamination control, independent review, reproducible evidence, and publication safeguards.

The Live Core concept is retained as an original research track, but it is separated from mission-critical systems and subjected to an explicit experimental sequence. This treatment allows genuine innovation while preventing speculative claims from weakening the credibility or safety of the programme.

The project can progress only through evidence-based gates. A crewed research installation is not justified until uncrewed systems have demonstrated reliable power, communications, environmental control support, resource characterisation, autonomous maintenance, emergency modes, and planetary protection over representative durations. Legal authorisation, registration, liability, and state supervision must be resolved before operations. The research zone remains an operational coordination mechanism and never a sovereign claim.

Under these conditions, SIRZ Mars can become a serious platform for energy research, planetary science, technology validation, and international scientific cooperation. Its success shall be judged by verified performance, responsible conduct, scientific value, and the quality of evidence it contributes to future Mars exploration.

# Appendix B. Minimum Crewed Readiness Evidence

A crewed mission shall not proceed without documented evidence that:

1. The landing site is characterised sufficiently for safe arrival, surface movement, construction, power deployment, and science planning.
2. Firm power has operated on Mars for the required duration and through representative environmental conditions.
3. Critical loads have verified redundancy and protected emergency reserves.
4. Black start has been demonstrated under realistic fault conditions.
5. Habitat pressure, atmosphere, thermal control, water, fire protection, and refuge systems are qualified.
6. Dust exclusion and decontamination controls are demonstrated.
7. Radiation shelter and monitoring are available.
8. Communications and local autonomy can support safe operation during delay and interruption.
9. Medical capability, crew training, pharmaceuticals, and consumables are matched to mission duration.
10. Mars ascent and return assets are available, inspected, protected, and supplied.
11. Surface mobility and rescue capability are operational.
12. Planetary protection procedures, clean science areas, waste control, and environmental monitoring are active.
13. Spares, tools, repair data, and maintenance capability cover critical failure scenarios.
14. Food, water, oxygen, energy, and medical reserves include accepted contingency margins.
15. Legal authorisation, registration, liability, nuclear safety, communications, and research approvals are complete.
16. Independent review has found no unresolved risk that makes crew deployment unacceptable.
